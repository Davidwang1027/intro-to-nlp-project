At this point, Houston sends a series of the familiar beeps down the line to the remote station, in this case Goldstone in California. These beeps, known as Quindar tones, are used to send a signal from Houston to the remote station to operate a switch that places its signal onto the radio carrier going to the spacecraft. Given the possibility of interference on the line from Houston, it isn't desirable to have it permanently fed to the spacecraft. Instead, it should only be transmitted when CapCom keys his microphone. A separate circuit could be arranged to carry the switching signal but it is more cost effective to signal this switch on the same circuit and so switching tones are used. It may be noticed that alternate tones are of a slightly different frequency or pitch. A quarter-second burst of 2.525 kHz signals the switch to send Houston to the spacecraft. Another quarter-second burst of 2.475 kHz tone signals that Houston should be removed from the carrier.

In modern cameras, colour balance is set using a variable scale that relates to the characteristic colour of light that is emitted from a black body at a particular temperature. For example, if a scene in being lit by the Sun, whose surface temperature is about 5,500 Kelvin, then use of that figure in the camera would render a white sheet of paper as white when directly lit by the Sun. Another example relates to old-fashioned artificial lighting that was produced by passing electricity through a tungsten filament which then glowed at about 2,700 K. A white sheet of paper lit by such an incandescent light would only be rendered white by setting the camera to that value. If the camera were left at 5,500 K, then the tungsten-illuminated paper would appear very yellow. Colour temperature values from 2,000 K to 7,500 K are typical on a camera, allowing most lighting conditions to be accommodated. Not so prior to the advent of digital image sensors.
Handling such variation of colour in the days of photographic film was far less sophisticated. For the most part, colour films were formulated with an inherent colour balance that was meant for either exterior light (about 5,500 K) or interior light (about 2,700 K). However, it is possible to compensate for the incorrect balance of a film by using filters to make the incoming light be correctly balanced by the time it reaches the film emulsion. The disadvantage of this is that because more light is being cut out to achieve the balance, then it makes the film to appear to be less sensitive. Buzz is telling Mission Control that they will be using film balanced for interior light to shoot the view out of the window during re-entry.
